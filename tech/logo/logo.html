1. A joker logo.

‘‘Your logo,’’ Ogle said. ‘‘You designed your logo yourself.’’
      Again Aaron’s face reddened. He had, in fact, designed it himself. But he thought it was fairly professional, a lot more than the typical home-brewed logo. ‘‘Yeah, so what?’’ he said. ‘‘It works. And it was free.’’

[BUR 1995]

Every free software project needs a funny logo. Linux has Tux the Penguin, BSD Unix has a little dæmon, and GNU has a Gnu. I thought the Orson programming language [MOE 2014] needed a logo too, so I drew one. Here it is, in three different versions. You can click on any of them to see it in a larger size.

Original Orson logo No background

The Orson logo is a joker, intended to look like the jokers found in decks of playing cards. (Maybe his name is Orson.) I chose a joker because Orson uses types called jokers, each of which describes an infinite set of objects with some property in common. The joker balances two balls on the tips of his fingers. The ball on the left has a pair of parentheses to represent Orson’s transformation phase, in which computation is performed in an applicative style. The ball on the right has a colon-equal to represent Orson’s execution phase, in which computation is performed in an imperative style.
      I drew the original joker in India ink on white paper, then scanned it, and cleaned up the lines with Gimp [KYL 1999]. The result is the first joker that appears above. Then I added colors and the two balls with Gimp, resulting in the second joker. The third joker is like the second, but without a background. That may not be apparent if you look at it with a browser.

2. Other logos.

Here are more ideas for Orson logos. I haven’t drawn them, but maybe I will someday. I don’t know if they would conflict with anyone else’s logos.

  • Someone said that the programming language C++ [STR 1994] is ‘‘an octopus made by nailing extra legs onto a dog.’’ I don’t know the source of that quote, so I didn’t reference it, but I guess the dog is the programming language C [KER 1988]. Orson is like C with more abstraction, so maybe the Orson logo should be a flying dog, or a flying cow, or a flying pig.

  • Plato proposed a theory of knowledge based on what he called forms [RUS 1945]. Of course Orson is based on control structures that are also called forms. Maybe the Orson logo should be an ancient Greek philosopher. He’d be a bearded sage in a toga, with a band of laurel leaves on his head.

  • Charles Dickens wrote a novel called Little Dorrit that involves a bureaucracy in which people fill out forms [DIC 1970]. Maybe the Orson logo should be a stuffy, nineteenth-century Dickensian character in a top hat. He’d sit at a tall writing desk, with a bottle of ink and a pen.

  • The French word ours means bear [OHF 2005], so an Orson is a son of a bear. Maybe the Orson logo should be a bear cub.

  • Valentine and Orson is a French medieval story about twin brothers, abandoned in infancy. Valentine is brought up as a knight, while Orson is raised by bears in the woods. An engraving by Peter Bruegel the Elder depicts a scene from the story, in which the brothers are reuinted [KLE 1963]. It shows a hairy, bearlike man that might be a good logo, but it’s not clear if he’s intended to be Orson.

I’ve avoided logos based on famous people called Orson, because the language isn’t named after anyone. I also don’t want trouble with them or their estates.
